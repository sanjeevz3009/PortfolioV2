## A Bit About Myself/ Why Did I Spin Up a Personal Website

At this stage in my career, and life, everywhere I go, I am asked for my socials and people want to know a bit more about me. I will never be on social media, but I do have LinkedIn, which I give out to people when asked.

I needed a way to make myself publicly available and I feel ready to do so. I feel like what I have to share, will help a lot of people. I have gotten feedback from people I met at work, friends and total strangers how my story, achievements and advice have helped them a lot. Some of them are doing really well in life due to that spark they got from me and their honest hard work.

I thought by having a personal website I can control the content, and make myself publicly available in my own way. I.e., share my website to share my photos from photography work as I get asked for my Instagram, which I don't have, when I go on road trips with my cameras etc, share my website to network in my field, also like a diary for myself, where I can put out anything in a format I am happy with.

As you know by now my name is Sanjeev. You can get to know me a bit more if you scroll through the main home page. I am originally from Sri Lanka, and I arrived at London Heathrow airport at 2011. I have been in the UK for around 15 years. I ended up in the UK after fleeing from a civil war that was happening in Sri Lanka or the final war as we called it before everything went to hell/ current state of the country.

### Sri Lankan Civil War/ Life in Sri Lanka

I spent most of my childhood in Sri Lanka running into bunkers, hearing bombs drops, collecting shrapnels and other interesting things which I shall not share here, as that could be a separate post in itself. I wasn't in school mostly until I came to UK around year 5/6, so around 9-10 years old due to the war, and even when I was in school in Sri Lanka, we would get sent into the bunkers anyways. Not truly being in school was quite fun *I think*. I wouldn't know as we were never formally educated during that period mostly in Sri Lanka.

Trying to get visas to come to the UK was quite long process, literally (the lines and wait was actually long) and process wise. Thanks to god or whatever we hoped and believed in, we did get visas after a year or two of spending some time in a part of Sri Lanka where my people weren't too welcome back then (there is still a divide). Those years before coming to the UK was eye opening as I was raised in a heavily segregated environment as a kid. Kids went schools based on the language they spoke, ethnicity and based on the religion.

I ended up in a Christian or a Catholic Tamil boys school (forgot now which one), as they were quite open to accept anyone as long as you spoke Tamil. It was strict, and taught me a lot of discipline as the punishments were brutal 🙃. Spent my final years there before coming to the UK around 2011.

### Coming to the UK

Coming to the UK was a privilege, and it was similar to how it was portrayed in few of the movies I did end up watching in Sri Lanka (as the military typically controlled what movies you could watch etc, and if you are caught with certain movies, punishments were harsh). In the UK roads were not bombed to pieces, no bombs dropping, every where was clean compared to Sri Lanka, no shrapnels to collect, I didn't see soldiers with AK-47s and people seemed to be in peace and people seemed happy.

Then there was a whole debacle of living in Croydon, which was an life changing experience. All my parents/ my mum could drill into my head from the day we arrived at the UK is around education and if I didn't get 100% I am a failure. I guess she was paranoid I might end up like most kids in Croydon, which is either getting into gangs, selling drugs, getting kicked out of school or ending up in prison. I was rebel and still I am as I went my own path in a good way. I guess I didn't fulfill my mums dream of becoming a doctor or a lawyer 😆 She doesn't think typing on a computer all day is a real job, so there is that... but I guess she is not worried or concerned about me anymore, so I guess I am doing well.

### Skipping to the Nice Parts

Now skipping past the wars, coming to the UK, being in Croydon (which was heaven for me coming from a war zone, regardless of what people think of Croydon... it's not that bad people, don't believe everything you see in the news), adapting to the culture and integrating.

We shall skip to how did I ended up in the world of coding, software engineering and the reason you are on this website.

### Pilot

I always wanted to become a Pilot, fly planes. I was getting bombed by fighter jets in Sri Lanka, and it's a big thing to see one or to see a drone. We as kids would sneak out to the nearby military bases and sneak into the bases due to the fact that they were positioned in big fields, where you can spot drones, and sometimes fighter jets. Parents hated that as we should be in bunkers when you hear any noise of jets.

I've gotten fascinated with these fighter jets, and I always wanted to fly one, which if I would told my parents, they probably would of locked me in a bunker (there are different types open and closed bunkers) 😅 as those fighter jets racked up a lot of bodies in our community. My mum nearly died once because of it, but the bunker buster bomb they called it, didn't detonate, but it did collapse her bunker at her workplace, they had to dig the workers out.

Becoming a Pilot was a expensive career in the UK back then and for the most part still is but, there are some avenues to get into a fully funded commercial airline pilot programme. When I was in secondary school, you had self fund it. Nor my family or I had £100K lying around. Most remortgage their house and take a loan out to fund their kids for pilot programmes. My parents are successful in business and are wealthy now, where they can fund whatever my little brother (who was born here and can't get off TikTok for some reason) would like but for me that was not an option back then.

So I switched and focused in on the next equally important and passionate area I was always intrigued with in my life, which was technology. In Sri Lanka, the house we lived in didn't even have electricity, we fetched water from a well, neighbour's cow gave milk, we had food coming from farms, we grew some fruits and vegetables also. We rarely went into the city as that was unsafe. There was not much technology per se in Sri Lanka during that time where we lived. The wealthier households had electricity, TV and a landline.

## Why a static site?

I went with a SSG for this personal website. **[See my article on SSG, SSR, CSR, and ISR]({filename}/blog/yet-another-rendering-strategy-post.md)** if you want to  understand these concepts deeper (I wrote that blog post to re-learn the concepts).

I always like to start a project or have a tech-stack that's very vanilla or barebones and build from there.

I already see a good use cases for using some **Vercel products, Next.JS etc.** With the help of **v0**, me using **TailwindCSS** already, I can convert everything fast, have a headless CMS such as **Contentful** which I have integrated into a Python Flask application before, taking it to prod. I see my personal website tech-stack evolving as the use cases and requirements make it sensible to upgrade.

Yes, I know my requirements functional and non-functional already but I get a joy from building things very vanilla and then upgrading as it makes you appreciate the upgrades whether it's using a headless CMS like **Contentful with Next.JS**.

A lot of people over engineer or go straight to big heavy frameworks and never truly appreciate or can appreciate those frameworks as they don't know how an application would look without that set framework. When you go that route you lack the foundations, and depth you gain from building things vanilla and working your way up.

Sometimes I build the same thing again and again in different frameworks. I.e., a blog site in Flask and Django (see my GitHub repo) to understand how it differs truly.

I am trying to do the same for this personal website.
